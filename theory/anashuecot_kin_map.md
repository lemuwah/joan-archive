## 2. Core Kinship Principles (Niantic/Narragansett)

### **A. Matrilineal and matrifocal tendencies**
- Women often anchor kinship continuity.
- Land and relational obligations may pass through maternal lines.
- Colonial records frequently suppress or omit these structures.

### **B. Cross‑sibling responsibilities**
- Siblings may share stewardship roles.
- Responsibilities can shift across siblings depending on marriage, alliance, or loss.

### **C. Name suppression and fragmentation**
- Indigenous women may appear under:
  - English names
  - partial names
  - name variants
  - relational descriptors instead of personal names

### **D. Affiliation through marriage or alliance**
- Women often serve as relational bridges between families or groups.
- These ties may appear only indirectly in colonial documents.

### **E. Land and stewardship logic**
- Land use and stewardship may reflect kinship ties not explicitly recorded.
- Colonial deeds may show “anomalies” that make sense under Indigenous logic.

## 4. Documentary Anchors

### 4.1 Hough 1858 Court-Martial Records
- Contains **Anaftiawin** variant.
- Places her in a **Narragansett conflict context**.
- Suggests relational ties to other named Indigenous persons.

### 4.2 Pawtuxet Records Vol. VII (1682 Sale)

- Establishes her as a **legal and relational actor**.
- Connects her to the Greene sibling set.

### 4.3 Fones Record (1703 copy of 1672 deed)
- Gender pattern analysis suggests her presence in the family structure.
- May contain indirect relational markers.
