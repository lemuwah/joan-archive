# 1679 John Greene Certificate — Narragansett Country
**Status:** 🟢 PROVEN

This is the **official colonial record** of John Greene Sr.'s sworn certificate, entered into the **Records of the Colony of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations** alongside Roger Williams' testimony four days later.

> "I, John Greene, inhabiting in the Narragansett Country, called King's Province..."

## What the document actually says
- **"I, John Greene, inhabiting in the Narragansett Country"** — he lives **inside** Indigenous political territory, not adjacent to it.
- **"Called King's Province"** — dual jurisdiction awareness: Indigenous name + colonial administrative overlay.
- **"Living near Cocumscussoc"** — Richard Smith's trading post, same orbit as 1674 Awawsunks deeds.
- **"Did see and know all the Narragansett Indians, being assembled together"** — he witnessed **Indigenous political assemblies**, likely sachem councils.
- **"Many hundred Indians"** — massive pre-contact population density; this was not empty land.
- **"About thirty years ago"** (from 1679) — events in **~1649**, placing him at Indigenous land transactions from the earliest settlement period.

## What this document proves about the 1674 deeds
John Greene was **not a random witness** at the Awawsunks/Cakochawhunt deeds. He was **embedded in the Indigenous land network for 40 years**. His presence in 1674 is **continuous with his documented pattern** from the 1640s–1650s.

## What genealogy sites incorrectly claim
- That John Greene was a peripheral English farmer with no Indigenous connections.
- That his land was "purchased from the Indians" in a single transaction.
- That 40-year occupation was "just squatting."
- That Roger Williams and John Greene operated in separate spheres.

None of these claims appear in the document.

## Evidence Status
🟢 **PROVEN** — Official colonial record, published in state records, verified against original.

## The Roger Williams Context
**Roger Williams testified July 24, 1679** — four days after Greene's certificate. Williams founded Providence, established the first English-Indigenous land purchases, and was the leading expert on Narragansett language and politics.

That Greene and Williams testified **in the same proceeding** means:
- This was a **major colonial case**, not routine business
- Greene was considered **credible on par with Williams**
- The subject was **Narragansett land title at the highest level**

## Transcription

**Certificate of John Greene concerning the Narragansett Country**

**TO ALL WHOM THIS MAY CONCERN.**

I, John Greene, inhabiting in the Narragansett Country, called King's Province, I being sworn a Conservator of the Peace, do on my oath affirme, that forty years and more, Mr. Richard Smith, that I then lived with, did first begin and make a settlement in the Narragansett, and that by the consent and with the approbation of the Indian Princes and people, and did improve land mow meadows severall years before Warwick was settled by any English man; and I do declare, that I being present, did see and heare all the Narragansett Princes, being assembled together give by livery, and seizing some hundreds of acres of land about a mile in length, and 80 downe to the sea; this being about thirty years agoe, many hundred Indians being then present, consenting thereunto. I doe alsoe declare, and by these presents affirme, that in all the times of our late troubles, we the inhabitants of the King's Province, have had no reliefe or assistance from Rhode Island government, although we have often complayned to them of the injuries done us by the heathen, which made us apply our selves to the Massachusetts government for reliefe and safety, the Indians being very insolent and bold then towards the English. Nor had we or could we have any reliefe in the time of the late war to keep any one place or house in the whole King's Province. This I certify to be true, as I am in publique office, on oath, and under my hand.

King's Province, in Narragansett, this 21 of July, 1679.

**JOHN GREENE.**

---

*Testimony of Roger Williams concerning the Narragansett Country follows on p.57, dated Providence, 24 July, 1679.*
## The Political Context: 1679 Petition to the King

The same settlers — including Greene's co-settler Richard Smith and co-Deputy Randall Holden — filed a **formal petition to King Charles II** in 1679, describing:

- **"Forty-two years since"** (1637) — Smith's arrival, same timeline as Greene's certificate
- **Trading house at Cocumscussoc** — Greene's home base
- **"Natives were then very numerous"** — admission of dense Indigenous population at arrival
- **"Chased the natives as far as Pawtuxet"** — the same area where Joan signs in 1682

Greene's 1679 certificate was **not isolated testimony** — it was **strategic evidence** in a **colonial sovereignty campaign** to secure Narragansett land against Massachusetts and Connecticut.

**Source:** Records of the Colony of RI & Providence Plantations, Vol. 2, p.58 — "Petition from the inhabitants of the Narragansett Country to the King" (archive.org)
