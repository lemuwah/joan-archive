Why Joan?
One woman appears briefly in the historical record. In 1682 she is identified simply as Joan, wife of John Greene of Quidnessett. Beyond that, the documentary trail grows remarkably thin.

Printing presses were operating in Boston and Cambridge by the 1630s. Rhode Island courts were recording by the 1640s. Land disputes generated paper trails throughout Narragansett Country. John Greene fought for his land at every turn — petitions, boundary disputes, colonial authority. He only submitted to the Crown after they guaranteed his land. A man this litigious, this papered, had a wife invisible to English law for roughly forty years.

The quiet is extra loud.

  • Who was she? We know her name appears once: the 1682 division deed. Every other record — baptisms, marriages, church rolls, court testimony, dower claims, gravestones — is silent. For roughly thirty years, a documented man's wife is a ghost. That is not a research failure. That is a pattern.
  • Why the silence? English women of John Greene's social tier appear in dozens of records. Joan/e appears once. The statistical improbability demands explanation.
  • What if she wasn't English? The Narragansett hypothesis: Joan/e may be connected to the sachem lineage, possibly identifiable with Anashuecot named in the 1672 Fones Purchase deed. If she lived between two legal worlds, English records would not recognize her name, her marriages, or her children.
  • Why did her consent matter in 1682? English law required a wife's name on a land division. She appears because she had to — not because English record-keepers chose to acknowledge her. Her single appearance is the exception that proves the rule.

"His wife, in 1682, was named Joan, but nothing further is known about her." — F.L. Greene, 1894. That boundary has held for 130 years. This archive exists to finally break it.

The 4-John Firewall

The genealogical conflation of multiple men named John Greene is the primary structural error propagated by La Mance (1904) and repeated in subsequent literature. These four men are distinct individuals with no documented connection. The firewall is established by multiple independent primary sources.

Why the firewall matters

La Mance (1904) assigned Joan/e the surname "Beggarly" and identified her as the wife of Surgeon John Greene of Warwick. This is chronologically impossible on two independent grounds: (1) Alice Beggarly, Surgeon John's documented wife, died in January 1643/44 — 38 years before Joan/e's 1682 appearance; (2) Surgeon John's documented geographic sphere was Shawomet/Warwick, not Quidnessett/Narragansett, while Joan/e appears exclusively in Quidnessett documentation. La Mance provides no citations, manuscript references, or archival basis for the "Joan Beggarly" claim. Status: [INVALIDATED]

The question that haunts this archive

If Joan/e gave birth to nine sons across roughly 15 years (c.1650–c.1665), where are the birth records? Where are the baptismal records? Where are the church blessings, the midwife testimonies, the neighbor's accounts? In a colony where women's church membership was carefully tracked, where court testimony by women was routinely recorded, where dower rights were legally protected — every woman in this family is invisible. That is not a coincidence. That is a pattern that demands explanation.

How to Help — The Digitization Imperative

Why digitization matters for Joan/e

The records that could identify Joan/e are scattered, unpublished, or known only through copies made before fires and war destroyed originals. North Kingstown's records burned. The 1695 will that might have named her — gone. The original 1682 deed with her name on it — survives only on microfilm at the RI State Archives. The Absolom Counsellor affidavit — exists as a scan with tear damage near the critical mark area. Every day these records sit un-digitized, un-indexed, and behind paywalls is another day Joan/e remains a ghost.

This is not just about one woman. It is about every woman erased from colonial records because she lived between two legal worlds. It is about the mothers, wives, and daughters whose names were never written down in English — or whose names were written once and then forgotten.
