Who is Joan?

Joan appears twice. That is all we have.

March 24, 1681/2 (OS = March 1682 New Style) — Home-place deed — SK Land Evidence Vol IV: John Greene and Joan his wife grant 120 acres to Daniel and 60 acres to James, reserving a 30-shilling life estate to Joan, "or to her mother if she survive." May 19, 1682 — Pawtuxet deed — William Carpenter — NK Land Evidence Vol 1: John Greene and Joan his wife give "free & voluntary consent" to Carpenter. Witnesses: John Wickes, Samuel Gorton. This is the second time in 56 days English law reached for her.

Documented

Alive March 24, 1681/2. Alive May 19, 1682. Can convey title. Her consent is legally indispensable twice in 56 days. Her mother is living in March 1681/2 per clause.

Not documented — honest gap

No baptism. No marriage record for John Greene to any woman in England or RI. No burial. No probate. No dower claim after May 1682. No church roll. Last documented appearance: May 19, 1682. Death date: UNRESOLVED — not proven before Sep 25, 1685. Previous claim that Edward deed proves her death was overstated and corrected — Edward deed proves John Sr deceased by Sep 25, 1685 only.

Why a shadow until a legal document required her?

Where English women leave a trail, Joan doesn't — until title requires her.

For a man who appears in Massachusetts Bay, Connecticut, and Rhode Island records from 1637 to 1685, his wife is visible only when her title had to be extinguished. English law did not record wives unless their rights blocked a sale.

The mother clause is the clue: "or to her mother if she survive" — If Joan were daughter of Alice Beggarly (d. Jan 1643/44 per Arnold to Winthrop letter — Massachusetts Historical Society — Winthrop Papers Vol 4), her mother had been dead 38 years — clause legally meaningless. How did they accept her authority? In May 1682, two respected men — John Wickes and Samuel Gorton — witness her "free & voluntary consent" as sufficient to clear title to Pawtuxet. That acceptance implies recognized authority — English law did not ask wives who held no interest. Her authority existed before English paper needed it.

Who is John Greene of Quidnessett — and how did he survive to give land in 1682/5?

How did John survive King Philip's War in Quidnessett and retain land rights to give to his sons?

John Greene of Quidnessett is not Surgeon John of Warwick. He is a separate man who lived at Quidnessett from the 1640s to 1685 — forty years in the middle of a contested border.

Act of Voluntary Submission — 1644 — Consent to be there: Richard Smith and other inhabitants of Narragansett submit to the Crown via the Act of Voluntary Submission — image: /images/actofvoluntarysubmission.jpg — Location: UK National Archives + RI State Archives — Copy in RI Colonial Records. John Greene operates under this framework — not as conqueror but as occupant by consent. This is why his deeds say "consent of the people" and name sachem grantors, not just English claim.

Trading land when it was illegal — and defending Narragansett rights when it could cost him everything: In the 1650s-60s, Massachusetts Bay and Connecticut outlawed private purchases from Narragansetts. John Greene continued to purchase via Fones (1672) and other deeds — e.g., Fones Purchase Deed #90 — Grantor Anashuecot "Chiefe Sachem and heire" — Location: NK Town Hall Vault, NK Land Evidence Vol 1, pp.22-23 — image: /images/1672_scan_p22_kin_cluster.jpg — This exposed him to arrest by Massachusetts.

Hough 1858 Narrative of Causes pp.173-191 — Newport Court Martial Aug 24, 1676 — John Greene appears to defend Awaswin (Awashowet family) and denies charges against Native allies: Original location: Brown University Library, Hay Library, Providence — Hough 1858 — Narrative of Causes — pp.173-191 — Also RI State Archives — Daniel Greene [PROBABLE ID] as witness — verbatim PENDING — CRITICAL per packet XV — If bond/surety, Lane 5 upgrades 75%→90%+. ID PROBABLE only — verbatim PENDING CRITICAL — request box below — Defending Narragansetts in August 1676, two months after King Philip's death, when anti-Native sentiment was lethal, could have cost him everything.

He still deferred to "Consent of the people" as his right to be there: In 1679 petitions — "forty years and more" — RI Colonial Records Vol 2 — 1678 Council Order Holden/Greene forty-years — image: /images/1678_council_order_holden_greene_forty-years_RI_records_62-63.jpg — Location: RI State Archives, RI Colonial Records Vol 2, pp.62-63 — and 1679 Petition Narragansett inhabitants forty-two years — image: /images/1679_petition_narragansett_inhabitants_RI_records_58-59_forty-two-years.jpg — Location: RI Colonial Records Vol 2, pp.58-59 — and 1679 John Greene certificate with Roger Williams testimony — image: /images/1679_john_greene_certificate_narragansett_RI_records_56-57_roger_williams_testimony.jpg — Location: RI Colonial Records Vol 2, pp.56-57 — John claims residency by consent, not conquest.

Survival is the evidence: Quidnessett was burned in King Philip's War 1675-76. Most English houses in Narragansett Country were destroyed. John Greene retained Quidnessett, still had title to convey in 1681/2 and 1685. That retention suggests standing with both sides — English courts recognized his deeds, and Narragansett sachems continued to convey to him (same kin group Womphoage, Ompamont, Quanuske, Mommocke in 1672 and 1688 — image: /images/1688-devilspurchase.jpg — Location: NK Land Evidence Devil's Purchase 1688 pp.20-21 — same cluster, 16 years later).
